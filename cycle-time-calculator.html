What is Cycle Time in Manufacturing?

Cycle time is the total elapsed time from the moment work begins on one unit to the moment work begins on the next unit at a given workstation. It is the fundamental pacing metric of any production line — directly determining throughput and output rate.

Cycle Time vs Takt Time vs Lead Time

MetricDefinitionDirection
Cycle TimeHow fast your process actually producesInternal — measured
Takt TimeHow fast the customer needs you to produceExternal — calculated
Lead TimeTotal time from order to deliveryEnd-to-end — experienced

For a balanced line: cycle time should equal takt time at every station. If any station's cycle time exceeds takt time, that station is a bottleneck — it cannot keep pace with customer demand.

Bottleneck Analysis — Theory of Constraints

Based on Goldratt's Theory of Constraints (TOC), the output of any production line is limited by its slowest workstation — the bottleneck. No matter how fast other stations run, the line can only produce as fast as the bottleneck allows.

  • Identify — Find the station with the longest cycle time
  • Exploit — Maximise throughput at the bottleneck (no downtime, no defects)
  • Subordinate — Align all other stations to support the bottleneck
  • Elevate — Invest to increase bottleneck capacity (more operators, faster machines)
  • Repeat — Once resolved, the next bottleneck appears — repeat the cycle

Line Efficiency Formula

Line Efficiency = (Sum of all cycle times) ÷ (Number of stations × Bottleneck cycle time) × 100%

A line efficiency of 100% means every station is perfectly balanced. In practice, 85–95% is excellent. Below 70% indicates significant imbalance — workload redistribution is needed.
