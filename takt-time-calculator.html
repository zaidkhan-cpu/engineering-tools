What is Takt Time?

Takt time is the maximum time allowed to produce one unit of product in order to meet customer demand. The word "takt" comes from the German word for a conductor's baton — it is the rhythm or beat that drives your entire production line.

Takt time is the foundation of lean manufacturing and is used to balance production lines, identify bottlenecks, and set the pace for every workstation on the shop floor.

The Takt Time Formula

Takt Time = Available Production Time ÷ Customer Demand

For example, if your shift has 480 minutes of available time (after breaks) and your customer needs 240 units per shift, your takt time is 480 ÷ 240 = 2 minutes per unit. Every workstation must complete its task within 2 minutes.

Available Production Time vs Scheduled Time

A common mistake is using the total shift length instead of the net available time. You must subtract:

  • Planned breaks and lunch periods
  • Team meetings and shift handovers
  • Scheduled maintenance windows
  • Any other planned non-production time

For an 8-hour shift with two 15-minute breaks and a 30-minute lunch, available time = 480 − 60 = 420 minutes.

How to Use Takt Time for Line Balancing

Once you know your takt time, compare it to the cycle time of each workstation:

  • Cycle time < Takt time: The station has spare capacity — good.
  • Cycle time = Takt time: The station is perfectly balanced — ideal.
  • Cycle time > Takt time: The station is a bottleneck — it cannot keep up. You need to reduce its cycle time or add resources.

Takt time analysis is the starting point for value stream mapping, line balancing, and any serious lean improvement project.

Takt Time vs Cycle Time vs Lead Time

  • Takt time — the customer's required production rate (external)
  • Cycle time — how fast your process actually produces (internal)
  • Lead time — total time from order to delivery (end-to-end)
